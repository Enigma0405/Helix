# Regulated System Design

Helix is designed with the strict constraints of heavily regulated industries in mind, particularly Pharmaceuticals, Medical Devices, and Aerospace.

While Helix does not claim formal certification out-of-the-box (as deployment environments vary), the core architecture adheres to the design principles required for compliance with **FDA 21 CFR Part 11** (Electronic Records and Signatures), **FDA 21 CFR Part 211** (cGMP), and **ICH Q9** (Quality Risk Management).

## Core Design Principles

### 1. Immutable Audit Trails
Every action within an investigation—from evidence upload to AI assessment generation to CAPA approval—is logged. Helix generates a cryptographic hash for key events, ensuring that the history of a deviation cannot be altered after the fact. 

### 2. Evidence Traceability
AI hallucinations are unacceptable in QA environments. Helix enforces a strict "EvidenceOps" pipeline where every claim generated by the Intelligence Layer must map back to a specific piece of uploaded evidence or a canonical node in the Organization Memory (e.g., a specific SOP or Equipment record). If it cannot be traced, it is not presented.

### 3. Human-in-the-Loop (Mandatory Approval)
Helix operates as a hyper-competent assistant, not an autonomous agent. The AI can draft a Root Cause Analysis and a 3-step CAPA, but it cannot close an investigation. The final execution strategy requires an explicit human review and approval gate, maintaining accountability.

### 4. Deterministic Organization Memory
Instead of relying on an LLM to "remember" how a sterile filter works, Helix stores the absolute truth in a relational PostgreSQL database (the Organization Memory). The AI only reasons against this hardcoded, deterministic truth, drastically reducing the risk of non-compliant suggestions.

### 5. Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
Investigation visibility, evidence modification, and CAPA approval are gated by user roles. Only users with designated QA permissions can approve a final CAPA, ensuring compliance with organizational hierarchies and Part 11 signature requirements.
